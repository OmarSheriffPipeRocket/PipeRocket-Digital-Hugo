An informational keyword is a search query where the user's primary goal is to understand something — not to buy, compare, or find a specific site. They're the most common type of keyword on the web, and they're widely misused by SaaS teams who expect informational content to generate pipeline directly.

## TL;DR

- Informational keywords are queries where the searcher wants to learn, not act — "what is MRR," "how does SaaS billing work," "what causes high churn."
- They're the highest-volume category of keywords and typically the easiest to rank for on a newer domain.
- Informational content builds topical authority and feeds future commercial rankings, but rarely drives direct conversions.
- The mistake most SaaS teams make is measuring informational content by pipeline instead of by the authority and rankings it creates for their commercial pages.
- Not all informational keywords are worth targeting — the ones that attract your ICP at the start of their journey are the ones worth building.

## What Is an Informational Keyword?

An informational keyword is a search query where the user wants an answer, an explanation, or a how-to — not a product page. The intent behind it is learning. "What is customer lifetime value," "how to calculate churn rate," "why do SaaS companies lose customers" are all informational.

Google classifies these queries differently from commercial or transactional ones and ranks different content formats for them. When you search an informational keyword, the results are almost always blog posts, glossary entries, educational guides, or explainer videos — not pricing pages or product demos.

- **Definitional queries:** "What is X" format. The searcher wants a clear explanation of a concept. Glossary entries and educational blog posts match this intent.
- **How-to queries:** "How to do X" or "how does X work." The searcher wants a process or method. Step-by-step guides and how-to articles match this intent.
- **Why queries:** "Why does X happen" or "why is X important." The searcher is building context or trying to diagnose a problem. Analytical, perspective-led content fits here.
- **Comparison of concepts (not products):** "Difference between MRR and ARR," "SEO vs SEM." The searcher is learning the landscape, not evaluating vendors.
- **Research queries:** "SaaS churn benchmarks," "average CAC for B2B SaaS." The searcher is gathering data to inform a decision, but isn't ready to make one yet.

Consider an onboarding SaaS built for mid-market HR teams. A query like "what is employee onboarding" is informational — the searcher is probably an HR manager learning the concept, not a procurement lead ready to evaluate software. That's a very different audience from someone searching "best onboarding software for remote teams." Both are worth targeting, but for different reasons and with different content.

What this means in practice: informational content rarely converts directly. Its job is to pull your ICP into your ecosystem early, build trust, and create the authority that makes your commercial and transactional pages rank later.

## Why Do Informational Keywords Matter for SaaS SEO?

The common argument against informational content is that it attracts people who'll never buy. That's true, and it's also missing the point. Informational keywords matter for two structural reasons that have nothing to do with direct conversion.

First, they build [topical authority](/glossary/what-is-topical-authority/). Google rewards sites that comprehensively cover a topic area across multiple pages. A SaaS that has deep, well-linked content on every concept in its category — churn, retention, MRR, NRR, expansion revenue — earns more authority for its commercial keywords than a site with only product pages and a few thin blog posts.

Second, they attract your ICP at the start of their journey. Someone searching "what is keyword difficulty" today might be searching "best SEO tools for SaaS" in four months. Informational content creates early brand touchpoints that matter in a long B2B buying cycle.

- **Topical authority building:** Covering the full breadth of your category's informational keywords signals expertise to Google and helps your commercial pages rank for competitive queries.
- **Early funnel reach:** Informational searchers are often future buyers. Capturing them before they're ready to buy creates recognition that influences the commercial decision later.
- **Internal linking foundation:** Informational blog posts and glossary entries create natural linking opportunities to your commercial and transactional pages, passing authority where it matters most.
- **Featured snippet opportunities:** Definitional and how-to queries frequently trigger featured snippets. Winning these gets your brand in front of searchers before they even click a result.
- **Lower competition:** Informational keywords are generally easier to rank for than commercial keywords. For a new SaaS domain, they're the entry point into organic search before the domain has enough authority to compete for harder commercial terms.

The nuanced warning here: not every informational keyword is worth targeting. A SaaS that sells expense management software doesn't need to rank for "what is a balance sheet." The informational keywords worth targeting are the ones that your specific ICP searches while they're still learning — the concepts directly adjacent to the problem your product solves.

## How Do You Choose Which Informational Keywords to Target?

The selection filter for informational keywords is ICP proximity. The closer a concept is to the problem your product solves, the more likely the person searching it is to eventually need your product.

A revenue intelligence SaaS should build informational content around forecasting, pipeline coverage, quota attainment, and revenue operations concepts. These are the topics their buyers think about. Building informational content around general business topics — leadership, productivity, team management — reaches a much broader and far less targeted audience.

- **ICP language mapping:** List the concepts your ideal customers discuss in sales calls, customer reviews, and support tickets. Those concepts become your informational keyword targets.
- **[Search volume](/glossary/what-is-search-volume/) vs. relevance:** A lower-volume informational keyword that your exact ICP searches is more valuable than a high-volume term that attracts a broad, unqualified audience.
- **Keyword difficulty assessment:** Informational keywords vary significantly in difficulty. "What is churn" is easier to rank for than "what is product-led growth" — both are informational, but the second has significantly more competition from high-authority domains.
- **Gap analysis:** Check which informational keywords your competitors rank for that you don't. These represent authority gaps in your topic cluster that are worth filling.
- **Supporting commercial intent:** The best informational keywords are those that naturally link to your commercial pages. If you can write a genuinely useful "what is X" post that references your comparison page as the natural next step, the content serves both the user and your conversion funnel.

A legal operations SaaS targeting enterprise procurement teams should build informational content around contract lifecycle management, redline review, approval workflows, and vendor onboarding — the concepts their buyers deal with daily. The informational content that doesn't convert to pipeline at least contributes to the cluster authority that helps their commercial pages rank.

## What Content Format Works Best for Informational Keywords?

Format matching is just as important as keyword selection. An informational keyword that you target with the wrong content format won't rank even if the content itself is excellent.

The right format depends on the specific type of informational query. Definitional queries ("what is X") are best served by glossary entries — concise, structured, with a clear definition upfront. How-to queries need step-by-step guides with clear action items. "Why" queries and analytical topics often need longer-form posts that build a genuine perspective rather than just describing a concept.

- **Glossary entries for definitional queries:** Short, clear, structured. The key is answering the "what" directly in the first paragraph and then adding depth. Google often pulls glossary-style openings as featured snippets.
- **How-to guides for process queries:** Step-by-step structure with numbered or bulleted steps. Each step should explain the action and the reason — not just what to do, but why it matters.
- **Analytical posts for "why" queries:** These need a real point of view. A post on "why SaaS churn spikes in Q1" that just lists generic reasons will lose to a post that takes a specific, defensible position with a contrarian angle.
- **Data roundups for research queries:** "SaaS churn benchmarks 2026" attracts searchers looking for numbers. A well-organised compilation of real data with clear sourcing ranks reliably for these queries.

The trade-off: glossary entries and short definitional posts are fast to produce and good for covering broad informational territory. Long-form analytical posts take more time but often earn more backlinks and carry more authority. A good SaaS content programme has both — the glossary provides the breadth, and the analytical posts provide the depth that earns links.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Should I put a CTA on an informational page?

Yes, but it needs to match where the reader is. A hard "book a demo" CTA at the end of a definitional glossary entry usually performs poorly because the reader isn't there yet. A softer CTA — "see how SaaS teams use this" linking to a case study, or "learn how to apply this" linking to a how-to guide — performs better. The best informational CTAs move the reader one step further down the funnel, not all the way to a conversion in one jump.

### 2. How do I know if an informational keyword is attracting my ICP?

Check the related queries and PAA boxes for the keyword in Google Search Console. If the surrounding questions are about the problem your product solves, you're in the right neighbourhood. If the surrounding queries are from a completely different audience — students, job seekers, or a tangential industry — the keyword is probably too broad to drive meaningful ICP traffic regardless of volume.

### 3. Can informational content rank for commercial keywords over time?

Sometimes, but it's usually a sign of something going wrong rather than something going right. If your informational blog post is outranking your comparison page for a commercial query, that's [keyword cannibalization](/glossary/what-is-keyword-cannibalization/) — and it's costing you conversions. The informational post is stealing clicks that should go to a page designed to convert. Fix it by restructuring internal links to signal which page should own the commercial query, and consider consolidating content if both pages are thin.

## The Bottom Line

Informational keywords are an investment in authority, not a shortcut to pipeline.
